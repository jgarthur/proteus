You’re at the point where the **parameter surface is too narrow relative to the hypotheses you want to test**.

Right now your exposed knobs mostly change ambient rates and broad economy terms: `R_energy`, `R_mass`, `D_energy`, `D_mass`, `T_cap`, `M`, `inert_grace_ticks`, `N_synth`, and mutation rates. But the spec’s actual selective landscape is also being shaped by fixed instruction costs, the protection/open model, hostile-action costs via `strength`, and the inert-fragment lifecycle. Those fixed rules are doing a lot of work.   

And your theory note is already telling you what the current regime is doing: small absorbers are too safe, the seed is mass-limited rather than energy-limited, and failed construction is biased toward absorb-heavy fragments that can persist for a long time.

So I’d split this into three things:

1. the **knobs you should add now**
2. the **metrics that matter most**
3. a **clean sweep plan** that lets you diagnose causes instead of just exploring blindly

## 1) Knobs I think you need to add

### A. Separate energy banking from mass banking

This is the single highest-value addition.

Right now one `T_cap` controls both energy and mass storage, and the theory note explicitly points out that `threshold = T_cap * S` lets tiny absorbers bank enough energy to ride out local deficits. That means “make reproduction necessary” and “allow mass accumulation” are entangled in one knob. 

Add:

* `T_cap_energy`
* `T_cap_mass`

Optional but useful later:

* `T_cap_energy_empty`
* `T_cap_mass_empty`
* `T_cap_inert_multiplier`

Why this matters:

* You want to be able to make tiny stationary absorbers less safe **without** also killing multi-cycle mass accumulation for plausible replicators.
* This lets you reduce energy hoarding while still allowing mass stockpiles for construction.

If you add only one new family of knobs this week, add this one.

---

### B. Split maintenance by program state

Right now maintenance is basically `M` for live, `0` for inert under grace, and `M` again for abandoned inert. That is elegant, but too rigid for diagnosis. The theory note says abandoned fragments can still persist for hundreds of ticks even in the lower-bound no-scavenging case.

Add:

* `M_live`
* `M_inert_active`
* `M_inert_abandoned`

Optional later:

* `M_abandoned_size_exponent`
* `abandoned_decay_bonus`

Why this matters:

* It lets you test whether the problem is specifically “debris sticks around too long” versus “live programs are too safe.”
* It gives you a softer alternative to adding hard dissolution or senescence.

My default expectation: `M_inert_abandoned > M_live` will be a very informative axis.

---

### C. Expose per-instruction costs for the metabolically central actions

The spec currently hardcodes a bunch of crucial costs: `absorb` 0, `appendAdj` base 1 plus 1 mass, `takeM` 1, `move` 1, `boot` 0, `synthesize` base 1 plus `N_synth`, hostile costs proportional to target strength, etc. Those are not incidental constants; they are part of the ecology.

You do **not** need every opcode tunable immediately. You need the ones that define the reproduction/metabolism/predation funnel:

Add at minimum:

* `cost_absorb`
* `cost_takeM`
* `cost_append_base`
* `cost_append_mass`
* `cost_boot`
* `cost_move`
* `cost_synthesize_extra` or keep `N_synth`
* `cost_takeE_base`
* `cost_delAdj_base`

And for hostile actions:

* `hostile_strength_scale`
  or
* `cost_takeE_scale`
* `cost_delAdj_scale`

Why this matters:

* Right now stationary harvesting is very cheap, booting fragments is free, and movement is costly enough that it is plausibly selected against unless it buys something real.
* Without exposing these, you can only adjust the bath, not the organism’s local decision economics.

My strong suggestion: even if you do not expose a full cost table, at least expose the six listed above.

---

### D. Add knobs for what hostile actions actually return

Right now `takeM` takes half of loose mass, `takeE` takes half of free energy, and `delAdj` converts one deleted instruction into one mass for the attacker, while hostile costs depend on target strength. That means predation pressure depends on several fixed ratios simultaneously.

Add:

* `takeM_fraction`
* `takeE_fraction`
* `delAdj_mass_yield`

Optional:

* `takeM_can_take_instructions` as a boolean or mode later, but that is more of a rule change than a tuning knob

Why this matters:

* It lets you test whether predators/scavengers can actually clear plant junk and crowded fronts, instead of just poking them expensively.
* It cleanly separates “predation impossible” from “predation viable but not selected.”

---

### E. Add a knob for the strength/cost coupling

Right now target strength is `min(program_size, free_energy)`, and hostile-action costs depend on target strength. That is a meaningful design choice, but it can also make energy-rich stationary organisms expensive to attack.

Add one of:

* `strength_energy_scale` where `strength = min(size, floor(free_energy / strength_energy_scale))`
* or `hostile_cost_scale` multiplying target strength in hostile costs
* or `strength_mode` with a few discrete forms

Why this matters:

* This is the cleanest way to test whether “plants are hard to clear because they are rich” is a real binding issue.
* It gives you leverage on predation without rewriting the whole combat model.

This is more important than it looks.

---

### F. Add explicit environmental forcing knobs

Your own spec/future-directions note and theory note both point toward stationary environments under-selecting reproduction. You want this exposed as a tuning surface, not only as a future redesign.

Add:

* `R_energy_amp`
* `R_energy_period`
* `R_mass_amp`
* `R_mass_period`
* `energy_mass_phase_offset`

Optional later:

* `spatial_patch_scale`
* `spatial_patch_amplitude`
* `spatial_temporal_noise_seed`

Why this matters:

* This is the cleanest endogenous way to ask whether “persistence is too easy” is the primary issue.
* It is much more diagnostic than just lowering average `R_energy`.

---

### G. Expose open/protection semantics as knobs

The protection model is central: `absorb` opens the cell, `nop` opens it, failure opens it, inert is always open, and protected cells resist `takeM`, `takeE`, `delAdj`, `writeAdj`, and occupied-cell `appendAdj`. 

Add:

* `absorb_opens_cell` (bool)
* `nop_opens_cell` (bool)
* `failed_payment_opens_cell` (bool)
* `immediate_limit_halt_opens_cell` (bool or fixed)
* maybe `open_duration_ticks` if you want one more degree of freedom

Why this matters:

* It lets you test whether the metabolism-vulnerability tradeoff is helping or just pinning the ecology in a weird place.
* It also helps you later if communication via `emit`/`absorb` becomes parasitic due to heading overwrite and forced openness.

I would not start here, but I would definitely add it.

---

### H. Add one spatial-freeze escape knob later, not now

Your spec already notes that `move` only works into empty cells, so dense regions can freeze. 

Later, add one of:

* `move_can_swap`
* `move_can_displace_inert`
* `move_can_merge_into_empty_only` mode enum

But I would not do this first. Your theory note is probably right that movement is not the first-order issue yet. 

---

### I. Add one anti-complexity knob later

This is not a “first batch” item, but it is real. The spec itself flags that larger programs pay more maintenance while still getting one 1-tick action per tick. 

Later, expose one of:

* `maintenance_size_exponent`
* `actions_per_tick_by_size`
* `queued_action_slots_by_size`

That is a bigger design decision, so I would hold it until the reproduction ecology is healthier.

## 2) Which metrics are most critical

Your current metric list is already quite good. The problem is not lack of signal in general. It is that the most important missing signals are the ones that tell you **where the funnel is failing**.

The four most important metric families are:

### A. A true reproduction funnel

This is the most important one.

You already have pieces of it:

* `append_adj_attempts`
* `append_adj_successes`
* `boot_attempts`
* `boot_successes`
* boot survivor shares
* exact-parent-match share at 10
* abandonment counters

What you still need is the funnel in a way that separates “construction reached plausibility” from “construction got booted” from “booted descendants were actually viable.”

Add:

* `offspring_reached_size_ge_parent`
* `offspring_reached_size_ge_parent_before_boot`
* `size_at_boot` histogram / quantiles
* `writes_received_at_boot` histogram
* `writes_received_at_abandonment` histogram
* `boot_exact_parent_match_share_50`
* `boot_exact_parent_match_share_100`
* `boot_nontrivial_survivor_share_50`
* `boot_nontrivial_survivor_share_100`

Why this matters:

* Right now “motif looks replicator-like” and even “got booted” are still weak proxies.
* The most useful decision boundary is:

  * are they failing before full construction?
  * after full construction but before boot?
  * after boot but before persistence?

That tells you which knob family is relevant.

---

### B. Failure reason breakdowns

This is probably the single highest-value instrumentation upgrade.

Right now `append success rate` and `boot success rate` are not enough. You need reasons.

Add for `appendAdj` failures:

* insufficient base energy
* insufficient mass
* protected occupied target
* lost conflict
* target state bucket: empty / inert / live-protected / live-open

Add for `boot` failures:

* target empty
* target already live
* lost conflict

Add for `move` failures:

* target occupied
* lost conflict if relevant later
* insufficient energy if that can happen separately

Why this matters:

* If append failures are mostly “insufficient mass,” that is one story.
* If they are mostly “target no longer empty/protected,” that is a spatial targeting story.
* If boots fail mostly because targets are already live, that is a very different ecology from “construction never got anywhere.”

This one is non-negotiable.

---

### C. Resource flow ledgers

You need explicit energy and mass flux accounting. Your theory note is fundamentally about economy. So measure economy directly.

Add per tick:

Energy inflows:

* background radiation arrived
* background radiation absorbed
* directed radiation captured
* `takeE` gained

Energy outflows:

* base costs by opcode
* additional costs by opcode
* maintenance
* decay
* failed-cost attempts if they consume anything
* energy transferred via `giveE`

Mass inflows:

* ambient mass arrived
* `takeM` gained
* `synthesize` produced
* `del`/`delAdj` recovered
* `giveM` received

Mass outflows:

* `appendAdj` spent
* maintenance fallback-to-mass
* decay
* `giveM` sent

Why this matters:

* This will tell you whether the world is fundamentally energy-surplus/mass-tight, and whether that changes under new knobs.
* It also lets you see whether abandoned fragments are becoming a major energy sink or mass reservoir.

This is more useful than another dozen phenotype shares.

---

### D. Fragment burden metrics

You already have:

* inert counts
* abandoned inert counts
* max inert wait
* booted-from-abandoned share

Good. Now make them size- and biomass-aware.

Add:

* fraction of total instructions in abandoned inerts
* fraction of total free energy held by abandoned inerts
* fraction of total free mass held by abandoned inerts
* size distribution of abandoned inerts
* lifetime distribution of abandoned inerts
* opcode concentration / absorb-share specifically within abandoned inerts
* share of booted-from-abandoned that are size 1–2, 3–4, 5+

Why this matters:

* The theory note says debris can remain ecologically relevant for hundreds of ticks and may be biased toward absorb-heavy fragments. Measure that directly.

## The next-most-important metrics

After those four, I would prioritize:

### E. Birth/death/turnover metrics

You have births implicitly. You need deaths explicitly.

Add:

* live births per tick by `birth_kind`
* inert births per tick
* live deaths per tick
* inert deaths per tick
* deaths by cause bucket:

  * maintenance energy exhaustion
  * maintenance mass exhaustion
  * tail erosion to zero
  * explicit deletion to zero

Why this matters:

* You need to know whether the world is high-turnover or a cemetery of durable junk.

---

### F. Empty-neighbor / target-availability metrics

This is the cheapest way to test the “crowding blocks replication” story.

Add:

* mean empty-neighbor count for live programs
* mean empty-neighbor count for putative builders
* fraction of append attempts aimed at empty cells
* fraction aimed at inert targets
* fraction aimed at live protected targets
* local occupancy around successful vs failed builders

Why this matters:

* It tells you whether the bottleneck is ecological crowding rather than pure economy.

---

### G. A shallow lineage metric

You said you do not yet have full genealogy. Full genealogy is not required to get most of the value.

Add a cheap lineage proxy:

* for exact-parent-match boots, propagate a `lineage_id`
* track lineage depth
* track active descendants by lineage
* compute `R_exact_10`, `R_exact_50`, `R_exact_100`

If exact matching is too strict, also do a relaxed version for “same size and small Hamming distance” later.

Why this matters:

* It is the shortest path to a real “this thing is reproducing over generations” metric.

## 3) A clear plan of action

Here is the plan I’d actually run.

### Phase 0: add the minimum instrumentation first

Before doing a giant parameter search, add:

* failure reason breakdowns
* resource flow ledgers
* reproduction funnel completion buckets
* fragment burden by biomass/resources

If you skip this, parallel sweeps will give you lots of pictures but weak inference.

---

### Phase 1: add the minimum new knobs

Implement, in this order:

1. `T_cap_energy`, `T_cap_mass`
2. `M_live`, `M_inert_abandoned`
3. central instruction costs:

   * `cost_absorb`
   * `cost_append_base`
   * `cost_append_mass`
   * `cost_boot`
   * `cost_move`
4. `hostile_cost_scale` or `strength_energy_scale`
5. simple temporal forcing:

   * `R_energy_amp`, `R_energy_period`
   * `R_mass_amp`, `R_mass_period`

That is enough to test the main hypotheses without a huge rewrite.

---

### Phase 2: run three orthogonal sweep families, not one giant mixed sweep

Do not sweep everything at once. You want interpretable contrasts.

#### Sweep family A: persistence pressure

Goal: test whether small stationary absorbers are simply too safe.

Vary:

* `T_cap_energy`
* `M_live`
* maybe `R_energy_amp`

Keep mass-side knobs fixed.

Primary readouts:

* absorb-only / absorb-dominated share
* abandoned fragment burden
* boot survivor 10/50/100
* death rate / turnover
* exact-match offspring survival

Interpretation:

* If reproduction improves mainly here, weak negative selection on persistence was the main problem.

---

#### Sweep family B: reproduction accessibility

Goal: test whether replication is still too hard even when survival is less cushy.

Vary:

* `T_cap_mass`
* `R_mass`
* `N_synth` or `cost_synthesize_extra`
* `cost_append_base`
* `cost_append_mass`
* `cost_boot`

Primary readouts:

* append failure reason breakdown
* full-size-before-boot share
* size-at-boot distribution
* exact-parent-match boot share
* exact-match 50/100 survival

Interpretation:

* If offspring now routinely reach full size and boot successfully, the main bottleneck was mass/reproduction accessibility.

---

#### Sweep family C: fragment cleanup / predation pressure

Goal: test whether the ecology is being clogged by inert junk and rich plants.

Vary:

* `M_inert_abandoned`
* `hostile_cost_scale` or `strength_energy_scale`
* `takeM_fraction`
* `takeE_fraction`
* `delAdj_mass_yield`

Primary readouts:

* fraction of instructions/resources in abandoned inerts
* booted-from-abandoned share
* abandoned inert lifetime
* live/inert biomass ratio
* predation opcode usage if you track execution frequencies

Interpretation:

* If this family helps most, “failed replication manufactures plants” and “junk is too durable” were major bottlenecks.

---

### Phase 3: only after that, test seasonal forcing seriously

If A–C still do not produce persistent multigenerational replication, then run forcing experiments.

Vary:

* `R_energy_amp`, `R_energy_period`
* optionally `R_mass_amp`, `R_mass_period`
* maybe opposite-phase energy/mass cycles

Primary readouts:

* whether lineages spread before winter
* whether tiny absorbers crash during cold phases
* whether exact-copy reproduction number rises

Interpretation:

* If seasonality is the first thing that really works, your substrate probably needs environmental nonstationarity to make spreading worth it.

---

### Phase 4: only then revisit movement and anti-complexity

If you start seeing real replication but crowded worlds stall:

* add target-availability metrics if not already there
* then consider spatial-freeze knobs

If you start seeing only tiny lean replicators:

* then revisit maintenance scaling / capability scaling

Those are second-order problems compared to the current ecology.

## How to decide what changed after each sweep

Use this decision rule:

* If **append failures are mostly mass-related**, tune mass access / synthesis / append cost.
* If **append failures are mostly target-state related**, tune crowding / empty-target access / movement later.
* If **full-size offspring are common but boot survival is poor**, the problem is post-boot ecology, not construction.
* If **abandoned inert biomass stays high across all runs**, fragment cleanup is a central bottleneck.
* If **stationary absorbers remain dominant unless you add forcing or lower energy banking**, persistence pressure is too weak.
* If **exact-match 10 improves but 50/100 does not**, you have construction working without lineage stability.
* If **exact-match depth rises only under seasonal forcing**, the world probably needs nonstationarity.

## My strongest recommendation

If you want the highest information gain per unit implementation effort:

**Add these first:**

* `T_cap_energy`, `T_cap_mass`
* `M_inert_abandoned`
* failure reason breakdowns
* energy/mass flow ledgers
* size-at-boot / full-size-before-boot funnel metrics

That combo will tell you very quickly whether you are mostly fighting:

* overly safe plants,
* overly durable debris,
* or genuinely inaccessible reproduction.

**tl;dr:** Your current tuning surface is too coarse. Add knobs that separately control energy banking, mass banking, abandoned-fragment maintenance, central instruction costs, hostile-action economics, and environmental forcing. Prioritize metrics that turn reproduction into a causal funnel and economy into a ledger: failure reasons, resource flows, size-at-boot, full-size-before-boot, and fragment burden by biomass/resources. Then run three orthogonal sweep families: persistence pressure, reproduction accessibility, and fragment cleanup. That will tell you what is actually binding instead of just generating more runs.

**Epistemic confidence:** high on the instrumentation and knob-priority recommendations; medium-high on the sweep ordering; medium on which knob family will prove most decisive in practice.
